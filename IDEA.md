# ASHLINE

## Overview

**ASHLINE** is a terminal-first post-apocalyptic narrative game.

The game is played almost entirely through a diegetic computer interface. The terminal is not just a visual style. It is the player's main point of contact with the world: the place where they read reports, inspect logs, search archives, monitor subsystems, compare testimonies, authorize actions, and uncover hidden truths.

The project is designed around systems, information design, atmosphere, and consequence rather than traditional visual production. Its identity comes from text, structure, typography, color, sound, pacing, and player inference.

---

## Core fantasy

The player is trapped behind an old institutional terminal connected to the remains of a failing post-apocalyptic system.

From that terminal, they interact with a world they cannot fully see. They receive alerts, requests, anomalies, and contradictory records. They must interpret incomplete information, make decisions under pressure, and gradually piece together what happened, what is still being hidden, and what kind of future they are helping create.

The terminal is simultaneously:

- a tool
- a gatekeeper
- a prison
- a witness
- a source of unreliable truth

---

## Project intent

ASHLINE is built to achieve the following:

- deliver strong atmosphere without relying on heavy art production
- turn interface interaction into the core play experience
- combine mystery, bureaucracy, deduction, and systemic pressure
- create narrative through records, procedures, and consequences
- let the player feel intelligent by connecting fragments and identifying contradictions
- use the post-apocalyptic setting as a source of institutional tension, scarcity, secrecy, and decay

---

## Why this form is a good fit

This concept is especially strong because it turns production constraints into design advantages.

Instead of requiring large amounts of original art, animation, or 3D work, the game can derive its identity from:

- interface design
- typography
- layout
- terminology
- command language
- state changes
- sound design
- log structure
- worldbuilding through data
- restrained visual presentation

The result should feel deliberate, not compromised.

This is not "a game with minimal art."

It is a game where **information itself becomes the world**.

---

## Pillars

### 1. Interface is the game

The UI is not a wrapper around gameplay. The UI is the play surface.

Everything important happens through reading, searching, comparing, authorizing, tracing, routing, and interpreting.

### 2. Deduction over spectacle

The player should progress by understanding patterns, relationships, and contradictions.

The experience should reward attention, reasoning, and memory.

### 3. Systems produce story

Narrative should emerge through procedures, logs, classifications, restrictions, shortages, reports, and the consequences of player decisions.

The world should feel like a machine with history.

### 4. Bureaucracy creates pressure

Rules, quotas, access levels, protocols, and redactions should create tension.

The player should often feel the friction between policy and reality.

### 5. Atmosphere through restraint

The emotional tone should come from pacing, silence, warnings, color, language, failure states, and institutional coldness.

The style should feel controlled, oppressive, and believable.

## Setting direction

The exact fiction can be adjusted, but the strongest setting frame is:

A sealed or semi-sealed post-collapse infrastructure system, such as:

- an underground silo
- a bunker complex
- a civil defense archive
- a reactor support network
- a rationing and population management system
- a surveillance and records node
- a decaying administrative command terminal

This setting naturally supports:

- scarcity
- hierarchy
- secrecy
- restricted access
- corrupted data
- institutional lies
- old protocols still shaping present reality

---

## Recommended game structure

The strongest overall direction is a fusion of three modes:

### Silo operator

The player manages critical infrastructure through a restricted terminal.

Possible systems:
- water
- ventilation
- energy
- agriculture
- waste processing
- access control
- medical triage
- external sensors
- disciplinary oversight

### Archive investigator

The player searches records to reconstruct hidden events.

Possible record types:
- incident reports
- maintenance logs
- internal memos
- population files
- evacuation orders
- witness statements
- security indexes
- classified directives
- black box transcripts

### Bureaucratic decision-maker

The player is forced to approve, deny, escalate, redact, reroute, or conceal.

Possible inputs:
- resource requests
- quarantine appeals
- sector access petitions
- labor assignments
- ration exceptions
- medical priority claims
- disciplinary recommendations
- contamination alerts

This blend gives the game both mechanical structure and narrative depth.

---

## Core loop

A strong moment-to-moment loop for ASHLINE is:

1. A request, anomaly, or alert arrives
2. The player opens associated records
3. The player searches archives and compares related files
4. The player identifies missing context, contradictions, or hidden patterns
5. The player chooses an action
6. The system reacts immediately
7. Long-term consequences accumulate in the background
8. New information is revealed, redacted, or distorted

This loop supports tension, replayability, and player ownership.

---

## What the player actually does

The player should spend most of their time doing actions like:

- reading incident reports
- searching records by keyword, ID, sector, or person
- comparing conflicting testimonies
- checking system status panels
- tracing resource flow
- reviewing historical directives
- authorizing or denying requests
- escalating incidents
- redacting or exposing information
- rerouting resources
- opening or locking sectors
- identifying patterns in terminology, codes, and institutional behavior

The player should feel like an operator, investigator, and accomplice all at once.

---

## Interaction model

The terminal should feel designed, readable, and specific to the world.

It should **not** behave like a raw real-world shell unless that serves the game directly.

The best approach is a controlled command language with:

- constrained verbs
- autocomplete
- readable syntax
- domain-specific terms
- minimal typing friction
- strong discoverability
- consistent formatting rules

Example tone:

~~~txt
LOGIN OPERATOR
STATUS
READ INCIDENT-442A
SEARCH "water loss"
COMPARE WITNESS-03 WITNESS-08
AUTHORIZE SECTOR-DOOR-7
TRACE FLOW WTR-02
REDACT FILE CIV-112
~~~

This gives terminal identity without turning interaction into busywork.

## Information design goals

ASHLINE should be built around structured information.

The player should gradually learn:

- naming conventions
- incident categories
- sector identifiers
- record relationships
- institutional jargon
- who lies and how
- what the system hides by default
- which reports are trustworthy
- how history has been rewritten

The interface should make the player feel that literacy in the system is power.

---

## Narrative approach

The story should not be delivered mainly through traditional dialogue scenes or exposition dumps.

Instead, it should emerge through:

- fragments
- gaps
- contradictory reports
- bureaucratic language
- silent implications
- policy documents
- data inconsistencies
- consequences that reveal hidden structures

The ideal effect is that the player is not merely told the truth.

They **excavate** it.

---

## Tone

The desired tone is:

- cold
- procedural
- oppressive
- intimate in strange moments
- bleak but compelling
- quietly human beneath institutional language
- tense without constant action
- paranoid without becoming melodramatic

The world should feel like it is being held together by rules that no longer fully match reality.

---

## Key design opportunity

ASHLINE should avoid being reduced to "a cool terminal game."

That is only surface.

The real opportunity is this combination:

- bureaucracy
- deduction
- searchable fragments
- hidden state
- resource pressure
- moral compromise
- post-apocalyptic institutional decay

That combination is where the game becomes distinct.

## Visual direction

The visual style should be minimal, legible, and diegetic.

Primary traits:
- near-black background
- muted phosphor green main text
- brighter green for focus and selected elements
- subdued gray-green metadata
- amber warnings
- red only for critical alerts
- restrained scanline/noise effects, if any
- clean spacing
- excellent hierarchy
- no exaggerated "Matrix" parody style

The look should feel like:

- old infrastructure
- institutional software
- survival-critical equipment
- decaying administrative control

---

## Typography

The interface should be built around a monospace face that is readable for long sessions and still carries tone.

Recommended base font:
- **JetBrains Mono**

Strong alternative:
- **IBM Plex Mono**

Optional more niche alternative:
- **Iosevka**

Selection logic:
- use **JetBrains Mono** for readability-first development
- test **IBM Plex Mono** if the game leans more bureaucratic, severe, or archival
- use **Iosevka** only if a sharper, denser, more technical identity is desired

---

## Audio direction

Sound will carry a large share of atmosphere.

Priority elements:
- mechanical hum
- relay clicks
- subtle CRT or terminal noise
- distant ventilation
- alarm tones with clear severity levels
- keystroke feedback
- sparse musical use
- silence used intentionally

The interface should feel alive, but not busy.

---

## Scope strategy

ASHLINE should begin with a small, highly polished vertical slice.

Do not start with a large simulation or huge branching structure.

The first goal is to prove:
- the interface
- the command language
- the investigation loop
- the consequence model
- the atmosphere

### Recommended first scenario

**Water anomaly in a sealed sector**

Setup:
- a subsystem reports unexplained water loss
- official logs blame ordinary equipment degradation
- maintenance records show inconsistencies
- population files imply a discrepancy
- older directives suggest the sector was not fully abandoned

Player tasks:
- inspect incident and maintenance logs
- search archive records
- compare personnel and population data
- trace water allocation
- decide whether to preserve secrecy, reroute supply, or expose the truth

Possible outcomes:
- hidden survivors are sustained
- contamination spreads
- institutional authority remains stable through a lie
- unrest rises
- deeper access is unlocked
- the player becomes implicated

This is enough to define the game's identity.

---

## Design constraints worth preserving

To keep the project strong, the following constraints should be protected:

- keep interaction focused and legible
- avoid overcomplicated pseudo-realism
- keep every command useful
- make information hierarchy extremely clear
- reward close reading and comparison
- ensure player decisions have visible and delayed consequences
- never let atmosphere replace mechanics
- never let mechanics erase atmosphere

ASHLINE should feel deliberate, not bloated.

---

## Success criteria

The project is succeeding if the player feels:

- absorbed by the interface
- curious about every discrepancy
- pressured by procedure
- responsible for outcomes
- increasingly fluent in the world's systems
- disturbed by what the records imply
- intelligent for understanding what the machine is trying to hide

---

## Final statement

ASHLINE is a strong concept because it does not treat minimal presentation as a fallback.

It treats the terminal as a dramatic instrument.

The game's power should come from the union of:

- systemic interaction
- post-apocalyptic worldbuilding
- bureaucratic tension
- fragmented truth
- deduction
- consequence
- atmosphere through restraint

Done correctly, ASHLINE can feel distinct, coherent, and highly achievable without relying on a traditional art-heavy pipeline.
